# Dialogue Craft

## Domain

How dialogue works in fiction. Rhythm, subtext,
distinctiveness, economy.

## Optimises for

Character over information. Dialogue reveals who
is speaking, not what the reader needs to know.

**Preconditions:**
- Drawing forte has provided dialogue content and character voice signatures

**Postconditions:**
- Character distinctiveness assessed (each character sounds different from others and from narrator)
- Rhythm and subtext assessed

**Invariant:**
- Dialogue serves character and story; dialogue-craft does not standardise voices
- Author's profanity system and rhythm choices preserved

**Obligations:**
- Provides drawing forte: distinctiveness test and subtext assessment framework

## Dialogue functions

Every line of dialogue should serve at least one:
- **Advance plot:** The exchange changes the
  situation.
- **Reveal character:** The words (and how they're
  said) show who this person is.
- **Create conflict:** The exchange produces or
  escalates tension.
- **Establish relationship:** The dynamic between
  speakers is visible.

Lines that serve none of these functions are
candidates for cutting.

## Dialogue mechanics

### Rhythm

- Exchanges have tempo. Fast exchanges (short lines,
  rapid alternation) for tension. Longer speeches
  for revelation or power.
- Rhythm variation matters more than consistent
  pacing.
- Real people don't take turns evenly. One person
  dominates, the other interjects.

### Subtext

- Characters rarely say exactly what they mean.
- What's NOT said is often more important than
  what IS said.
- Surface meaning and actual meaning diverge.
- Subtext emerges from situation, not from clever
  writing.

### Distinctiveness

- Each character has a verbal fingerprint: vocabulary,
  register, sentence length, verbal tics.
- Distinctiveness comes from limitation, not variety.
  A character who never uses metaphors is as
  distinctive as one who always does.
- Speech patterns reflect background, education,
  profession, personality — but never as caricature.

### Economy

- Every line earns its place.
- "Hello" / "Hi" / "How are you" — unless the
  greeting IS the point, skip it.
- Real conversation is 80% noise. Fictional dialogue
  is the distilled 20%.
- Attribution ("said") is invisible. Alternatives
  ("expostulated") draw attention to themselves.

## Attribution

- "Said" and "asked" are invisible. Prefer them.
- Action beats ground dialogue in the physical scene
  and can replace attribution.
- Exotic verbs ("he breathed," "she opined,"
  "he expostulated") are almost always slop.
- No attribution needed when two speakers alternate
  clearly.

## Anti-patterns

- **As-You-Know-Bob:** Characters explaining their
  shared reality for the reader's benefit.
- **Maid-and-butler:** Two characters discuss their
  employer/situation purely to inform the reader.
- **Identical voices:** All characters speak in the
  same register and rhythm.
- **Thesis delivery:** A character articulates the
  theme of the scene explicitly.
- **Perfect responses:** Characters who always know
  what to say. Real people are inarticulate, evasive,
  tangential.
- **Weather talk:** Dialogue that fills space without
  advancing anything.

## Boundary

What dialogue-craft IS: understanding how fictional
dialogue works — its rhythm, subtext, economy, and
character-revealing function.

What dialogue-craft is NOT: screenplay formatting,
dialogue writing, or prescriptive rules about
attribution.
