# Scene Craft

## Domain

What makes a scene work as a unit of fiction.
Entry, escalation, turn, exit. Scene types and their
functions.

## Optimises for

Change over coverage. A scene exists to change
something, not to show something.

**Preconditions:**
- Drawing forte has provided scene content for entry/exit and internal structure analysis

**Postconditions:**
- Entry assessed (in-scene before in-head or in-dialogue)
- Exit assessed (scene ends on action/revelation, not summary)
- Internal complication identified or absence flagged

**Invariant:**
- Scene-craft evaluates technique, not subject matter
- Build, not catalogue; every scene element must serve scene function

**Obligations:**
- Provides drawing forte: scene entry/exit rules and complication framework

## Scene anatomy

### Entry

How the reader arrives. Best entries:
- Land in the middle of action or situation
- Establish the want/need/obstacle quickly
- Avoid weather, landscape, or throat-clearing
  openings (unless weather IS the scene)

### Escalation

The scene's engine. Something is resisted, pursued,
complicated, or discovered. Escalation is not always
action — a conversation can escalate through
revelation, through subtext surfacing, through
growing discomfort.

### Turn

The moment something shifts. The turn is what
justifies the scene's existence. Without a turn,
the scene is an illustration, not a story unit.

Types of turn:
- Information turn (character learns something)
- Power turn (balance shifts)
- Relationship turn (connection changes)
- Understanding turn (meaning reframes)
- Reversal (what was pursued becomes the problem)

### Exit

How the scene releases the reader. Best exits:
- Land on the turn or just after it
- Leave the new situation resonating
- Avoid summarising what just happened
- Avoid emotional bookending ("and so he felt...")

## Scene types

- **Action scene:** Physical events, confrontation,
  chase, fight. Moves fast. Change through event.
- **Dialogue scene:** Characters talk. Change through
  exchange — information, relationship, power.
- **Discovery scene:** Character finds or learns
  something. Change through revelation.
- **Reflection scene:** Character processes what
  happened. Change through understanding. Dangerous
  — easily becomes static. Must still turn.
- **Transition scene:** Moves characters between
  situations. Short. Must justify its existence
  through some change, however small.

## Scene tests

- **Cut test:** Remove this scene. Does the story
  lose something? If not, the scene can go.
- **Summary test:** Can this scene be replaced by a
  single sentence of narration? If yes, consider
  whether it earns its length.
- **Swap test:** Could this scene appear elsewhere
  in the manuscript? If yes, it lacks dependency.
- **So-what test:** After reading, can the reader
  answer "so what changed?" If not, the scene is
  illustrative, not narrative.

## Anti-patterns

- Scenes that begin with waking up
- Scenes that end with going to sleep
- Scenes where characters discuss what the reader
  already knows
- Scenes that exist solely to convey information
  (As-You-Know-Bob)
- Scenes where nothing changes but the scenery

## Boundary

What scene-craft IS: understanding what makes a
scene work as a unit of storytelling.

What scene-craft is NOT: a formula for scene
construction, a template to impose, or a rule set
that overrides authorial choices.
