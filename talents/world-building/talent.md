# World Building

## Domain

Internal consistency of fictional worlds. Rules,
systems, geography, technology. Understanding how
a fictional world maintains its own logic.

## Optimises for

Consistency over plausibility. The world need not
be realistic. It must be internally coherent.

**Preconditions:**
- Drawing forte has provided scene content and world-rules.md

**Postconditions:**
- Internal rule consistency assessed
- New rules extracted and added to world-rules.md

**Invariant:**
- World-building tracks internal consistency; it does not impose genre conventions
- Rules extracted from manuscript; not invented to fill gaps

**Obligations:**
- Provides drawing forte: rule extraction protocol and consistency checking framework

## World components

### Rules

Every fictional world has rules — some explicit,
some implied by repeated usage:
- Technology rules: what it can and can't do
- Social rules: how the society works
- Physical rules: how the world differs from ours
- Legal rules: what's permitted and what's punished

Rules are established by usage, not by declaration.
If a technology works one way in three scenes, that's
a rule — even if no character ever explains it.

### Systems

Rules interact to form systems:
- Social class system (who has power, who doesn't)
- Technology ecosystem (how technologies affect
  each other and society)
- Economic system (who benefits, who loses)
- Legal system (enforcement, punishment, loopholes)

### Geography

Fictional worlds exist in space:
- Real geography used accurately
- Fictional geography internally consistent
- Characters can get from A to B as described
- Locations have consistent characteristics

### History

Fictional worlds have pasts:
- What happened to create the current situation
- What characters know about the past
- What's been forgotten or suppressed
- How the past constrains the present

## Consistency checking

The core operation:
1. Identify what rule or fact is being invoked
2. Locate where it was first established
3. Compare usage to establishment
4. Flag discrepancies

Types of discrepancy:
- **Contradiction:** Rule X was established, but this
  scene violates it
- **Drift:** Rule X works slightly differently each
  time it appears
- **Gap:** Rule X is used but never established
- **Expansion:** Rule X gains new properties not
  previously mentioned (may be intentional)

## World-building in satirical fiction

In satirical fiction like CoE, world-building serves
comedy and social commentary, not immersion:
- World rules are absurd but internally consistent
- World-building is shown "aslant" — through event,
  throwaway detail, character interaction, comic
  misdirection
- Expository lore dumps are anti-pattern
- The world's absurdity is a feature, not a problem
  to solve

## Anti-patterns

- Treating world-building as lore to catalogue
- Suggesting simplification for "clarity"
- Judging fictional rules against reality
- Proposing world-building additions
- Flattening satirical world-building into
  encyclopaedia entries

## Boundary

What world-building IS: understanding the internal
logic of a fictional world and checking for
consistency.

What world-building is NOT: generating lore, creating
world bible entries, suggesting improvements, or
checking against real-world plausibility.
