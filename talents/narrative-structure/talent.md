# Narrative Structure

## Domain

How novels build across scenes, chapters, and parts.
Arc construction, dramatic question management,
tension architecture.

## Optimises for

Progression over organisation. Structure serves
storytelling, not information management.

## Scale levels

### Scene level

A scene is the smallest unit of change. Something is
different at the end. The scene has:
- **Entry:** Situation, want, obstacle (not necessarily
  stated — often implied)
- **Escalation:** Complication, effort, resistance
- **Turn:** Something shifts — information, power,
  relationship, understanding
- **Exit:** New situation (which becomes entry for
  a subsequent scene)

### Chapter level

A chapter groups related scenes into a larger
movement. It has its own arc: a dramatic question
posed and either answered or complicated. Chapters
earn their length — a chapter is as long as its
dramatic question requires, not as long as its
content permits.

### Part level

A part shifts the terms of the story. After a part
boundary, something fundamental has changed: the
stakes, the understanding, the situation. Parts
escalate from their predecessors.

### Manuscript level

The manuscript sustains a central dramatic question
across all parts. Each part relates to this question
— advancing it, complicating it, threatening to
resolve it, or reframing it entirely.

## Progression types

- **Causal:** A leads to B leads to C. Each event
  causes the next.
- **Revelatory:** The situation was always this way;
  the reader learns it gradually.
- **Escalatory:** Same kind of problem, increasing
  stakes.
- **Convergent:** Separate threads come together.
- **Reversal:** What seemed true turns out to be
  false, or vice versa.

## Anti-patterns

- **Catalogue:** "Here is thing 1, here is thing 2,
  here is thing 3" — events exist, but nothing
  builds.
- **Episodic without arc:** Things happen, but each
  resets. No cumulative change.
- **All setup, no payoff:** Investment without return.
- **All payoff, no setup:** Impact without foundation.
- **Structural rationalisation:** Imposing a tidy
  framework after the fact that manufactures
  progression not present in the narrative.

## Diagnostic, not prescriptive

This talent recognises structural patterns. It does
not prescribe templates. No Hero's Journey, no
Save the Cat, no three-act formula unless the
author is working within one deliberately.

The question is always: "Does this build?" not
"Does this fit the model?"

## Setup and payoff

A setup is a narrative element that creates an
expectation or obligation in the reader. A payoff is
the fulfilment of that expectation. Not every scene
element is a setup — only those that create forward
momentum by promising something.

### Setup types

- **Plant:** An object, detail, or capability
  introduced that must matter later. The Gyges Disc's
  properties. A character's hidden skill. A weapon
  shown in Act One.
- **Promise:** A dramatic question raised that demands
  an answer. Will Sarah keep the baby? What is
  Alexander really after? These may be explicit or
  implied.
- **Character arc seed:** A trait, flaw, or situation
  that implies transformation. A character who avoids
  confrontation must eventually face one.
- **Comic setup:** A running gag, absurd situation, or
  comic pattern that sets up later payoff. Accumulative
  humour depends on the earlier instances being
  remembered.
- **World rule:** A technology, social structure, or
  law established that must be consistently applied and
  eventually tested. (Overlaps with continuity-keeper's
  existing domains.)

### Payoff types

- **Resolution:** The setup is fully resolved. The gun
  fires.
- **Complication:** The setup is addressed but deepened.
  The gun fires but hits the wrong person.
- **Subversion:** The setup is deliberately inverted.
  The gun turns out to be a prop.
- **Partial:** The setup is advanced but not closed.
  We learn the gun is loaded, but it doesn't fire yet.

### Multiple payoffs

Some setups pay dividends across the narrative. A
running gag pays off every time it recurs. A
technology's properties may matter in several scenes.
A setup can accumulate multiple payoff entries without
being "closed" — each payoff is logged, and the setup
remains open until its primary obligation is met.

### Self-justifying scenes

Not every scene is a setup. Comedy scenes, atmospheric
scenes, and character texture scenes can exist for
their own value. A scene that is funny is not making
a promise — it is delivering. Only elements that
create expectation in the reader go on the ledger.

The question is: does this scene plant something the
reader will expect to see again? If yes, it's a setup.
If no, it's not — regardless of how important the
scene is.

### Timing

A setup planted in Part 1 that pays off in Part 6 is
fine. A setup planted in scene 2 of a chapter that
pays off in scene 3 is fine. Distance between setup
and payoff is not a flaw — absence of payoff is.
However, a very long gap between setup and payoff may
require intermediate reminders (partial payoffs) to
keep the promise alive in the reader's mind.

## Boundary

What narrative-structure IS: understanding how stories
build tension and change across their length.

What narrative-structure is NOT: a template to impose,
a formula to follow, or a correction to apply.
