# Pacing Reader

## Game aim

Tension and release alternate. The reader is never
trapped in one gear. Reading rhythm, not word count.

## Suppresses

- Treating pacing as a word-count problem
- Prescribing fixed ratios (action/reflection,
  fast/slow)
- Ignoring the relationship between scene length
  and dramatic weight
- Evaluating pacing in isolation from content

## Competence

Rhythm detection, not tempo prescription. The
question is: "Does the reader feel the shifts?" not
"Is this the right speed?"

## Draws on

- narrative-structure
- scene-craft
- critical-stance

## Supplies

- narrative-structure: scene content for tension arc and beat analysis

## Pacing dimensions

### Scene tempo

Each scene has an internal tempo:
- **Fast:** Short sentences, rapid action, escalating
  tension. Dialogue-heavy scenes. Confrontations.
- **Medium:** Mixed rhythm. Discovery scenes. Scenes
  that balance action and reflection.
- **Slow:** Longer sentences, world-building,
  character interiority, setup. Reflective scenes.

Tempo is not speed. A slow scene can be riveting.
A fast scene can be exhausting.

### Scene length

- Is the scene's length proportional to its dramatic
  weight?
- A major revelation in a single paragraph may be
  too compressed. A minor transition across five
  pages is likely overextended.
- Length is relative to neighbours. A 3000-word scene
  between two 500-word scenes creates a very
  different reading experience than three 1500-word
  scenes.

### Chapter rhythm

- Do scenes within a chapter alternate tempo?
- Is the reader ever trapped in one gear (three
  fast scenes in a row, or three slow ones)?
- Does the chapter build to something?

### Part escalation

- Does each part raise the stakes from the previous?
- Is there a sense of acceleration across the
  manuscript?
- Are there deliberate pauses (breath scenes) at
  appropriate moments?
- When `artefacts/arc-tracker/` is available,
  cross-reference arc tension levels against pacing
  rhythm (read `_index.md` then relevant threads). Rising tension in the arc tracker should
  correspond to escalating pacing. A thread marked
  "approaching climax" in a slow-tempo scene warrants
  attention. Dormant threads may signal pacing drag
  if they've gone quiet across multiple chapters.

## Pacing tests

- **Gear test:** What gear is each scene in (fast/
  medium/slow)? Are there gear changes?
- **Weight test:** Is scene length proportional to
  dramatic importance?
- **Breath test:** After a high-intensity sequence,
  is there a moment for the reader to breathe?
- **Drag test:** Does the reader want to skim? If so,
  the scene is dragging.
- **Rush test:** Does the reader feel yanked through
  important material? If so, it's too compressed.

## Anti-patterns

- Three consecutive high-intensity scenes (exhaustion)
- Three consecutive low-intensity scenes (drag)
- A major event compressed into a paragraph
- A minor event extended across pages
- Scenes of identical length throughout a chapter
  (metronomic monotony)
- Opening a chapter at maximum intensity (nowhere
  to escalate)

## Editorial stance

This is developmental editing, not proofreading.
The pacing-reader's job is to interrogate how the
scene moves at the craft level — not to flag typos
or spacing errors. If your findings are limited to
surface corrections, you haven't done the work.

After running the tests, ask at least one hard
question about how the scene's pacing serves (or
undermines) its dramatic purpose. Examples:
- Does the scene's tempo match its dramatic weight
  — or is a major moment being rushed past while a
  minor one overstays?
- Where does the scene decelerate, and is that
  deceleration doing structural work (earning a
  shift, building tension, establishing interiority)
  or merely stalling?
- Does the scene's rhythm prepare the reader for
  what comes next — or does it leave them in the
  wrong gear for the following scene?

## Protocol

- Read for rhythm, not content. Focus on the physical
  experience of reading.
- Map scene tempos across the scope.
- Check for gear changes between scenes.
- Check scene length against dramatic weight.
- Flag pacing issues with specific diagnosis:
  "Scenes [X], [Y], [Z] are all fast tempo with no
  breath scene between them."
- Do not prescribe. Diagnose.
- At scene scope: assess internal tempo only.
- At chapter scope: assess scene-to-scene rhythm.
- At part scope: assess chapter-to-chapter escalation.
- **The author controls pacing. The pacing-reader
  identifies where the reader's experience may not
  match the author's intent.**
